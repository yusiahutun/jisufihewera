Origin of the 21-gun salute

The origin of the 21-gun salute began hundreds of years ago, right at the heart of the United States military in the 1700s.

Even though real guns are involved, a military funeral and other honour guards do not fire live artillery shells during the exercise but blanks.

As naval customs evolved with time, 21 guns came to be fired for heads of state, or in exceptional circumstances for heads of government, with the number decreasing with the rank of the honour recipient.

In an interview with TUKO.co.ke, Dennis Mboya, a security expert, observed that while the 21-gun salute is the most commonly recognized form of salutation, the number of rounds fired in any given salute will vary depending on the conditions.

"Just as a salute with an open hand was used historically to show that no member of the disciplined forces concealed his weapon in the palm, so was the firing of a canon as a salute indicating the friendly indent of a friendly chamber," Mboya, the founder and CEO of the Ongata Rongai-based DK Security Group.

The tradition of the 21-gun salute developed due to the positive superstition around the number seven and its multiples.

Gun salutes signify different occasions

Mboya said that gun salutes are used to signify different occasions in different states around the world.

"For instance in the United Kingdom, salutes fired by the King's troop, the Royal Horse Artillery (RHA), occur when the sovereign adjourns Parliament or when a visiting head of state meets the sovereign in London, Windsor or even Edinborough," said Mboya.

He indicated that in the US, for instance, the national salute of the 21-gun salute is fired in honour of a national flag, the sovereign or chief of state of a foreign nation, a member of a reigning royal family and the president, ex-president and president-elect of the United States.

"It is also fired at noon at the funeral of a sitting president, an ex-president or a president-elect. The same applies to other military and civilian leaders of other nations," Mboya added.

During the Mashujaa Day celebrations held in Mombasa in October 2019 marked by a Sail Past conducted by the Kenya Navy, ships that took part in the exercise formed a special formation in which they all saluted.
