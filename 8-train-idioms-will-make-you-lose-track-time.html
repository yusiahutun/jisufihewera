8 train idioms will make you lose track of time

The long-awaited East Rail cross-harbour extension is now running, so trains and railways have been in the news a lot recently. Let's learn some train idioms to help us stay on track!

That train has left the station

Meaning: An option, choice or opportunity which is no longer available.

Example: I thought the art competition was still open for entries, but it's closed. It looks like that train has left the station.

Gravy train

Meaning: A situation in which someone can make a lot of money or gain many advantages for very little effort.

Example: Sam hopped on the gravy train when he got his new job. It's really easy and well paid.

Train of thought

Meaning: The way in which someone reaches a conclusion.

Example: Do you understand Harry? He went from talking about cats to saying we should go on holiday. I cannot follow his train of thought.

Train wreck

Meaning: A chaotic situation that is so bad or embarrassing, you can't help but watch.

Example: Lisa's group spent so long working on their project, but it was a total train wreck. I felt so bad for them when Justin's shirt caught fire.

The wrong side of the tracks

Meaning: A poor or less prestigious part of town.

Example: Ben grew up on the wrong side of the tracks, but he studied hard and later he became a doctor.

Lose track of time

Meaning: To not realise how much time is passing by.

Example: I met my best friend on my way to school. We chatted until I lost track of time, and so I missed my first class.

Track record Meaning: The past achievements of a person or an organisation. Example: This job needs someone with a good track record of getting things done.

Go off the rails

Meaning: To behave very badly for a period of time.

Example: Playing basketball helped Tim make sure he didn't go off the rails when he was younger.

Fun facts

The first trains were built in the 1800s, and they were powered by steam. Nowadays, trains are powered by different kinds of energy, like diesel and electricity.
