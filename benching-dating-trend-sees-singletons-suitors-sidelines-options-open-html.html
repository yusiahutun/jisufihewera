'Benching' dating trend sees singletons put would-be suitors on the sidelines to keep their options

You've been on a few dates with someone, you get on pretty well but it doesn't really go anywhere. However, the e-flirtations sporadically crop up a few months later just when you'd given up hope. Sound familiar?

This dating behavior, apparently spreading like wildfire among millennials, has been branded 'benching', by New York Times writer Jason Chen.

Someone might be on the fence about a potential suitor, so instead of getting rid of them, they'll keep their options open and literally put them on the 'bench' - where there might be several others waiting.

One female, 27, from London, told MailOnline how she had been a victim of 'benching'.

Explaining her situation, she said: 'I started seeing this guy in January who I met at a Christmas party. 

'We exchanged numbers, and started messaging etc the usual protocol. It took about a month of messaging before we met up due to busy schedules and we went on a few fun dates.

'At the beginning I wasn't really into it and was actually dating him to distract myself from another guy I had recently been obsessing over but as the dates carried on I started to think this guy was pretty cool.

'It's like he knew he had me in a good place because once I started liking him he took his feet off the gas pedal and stopped making an effort to hang out.

'Instead of messaging to see how I was, he'd send me an emoji on whatsapp (usually a hand signal) to check in on a Friday night. But really this was his way of saying "hey, I'm free tonight, let's hook up".

'He'd kept me sweet for a month or so and then I got bored and sacked it off.'

Chen notes that: 'Benching is just the modern incarnation of what we used to call leading someone on. But, as with so many formerly minor nuisances, it’s become comically frictionless in the smartphone era.'

Talking to several young people in New York, Chen discovered that they had all played the 'benching' card or been 'benched' by others. 

He concludes with a frank piece of advice: 'When benching happens, the old maxim is true. He’s just not that into you.'

Dozens of people have commented on the article, with many readers all too aware of the 'benching' culture.

One man said he sees nothing wrong with the process, writing: 'If you find yourself getting benched (or benching someone yourself,) you have the choice of being annoyed by it what it is not or embracing it for what it is.

'For me, it's a practice flirt or a warm up [conversation]. Each of us gets our ego's stroked by the exchange [and] neither walks away confused or angry.'

Another female reader highlighted that it's not just men practicing 'benching' and women do it too.

She revealed: 'Women can definitely be benchers and men and women can mutually bench. I'm currently "seeing" a guy where neither of us takes it very seriously.

'We meet up from time to time, we cancel plans on each other, we like each other but we're both open to meeting other people and seeing if something "better" comes along. I have no qualms and I don't think he does either. So women can definitely bench men.' 

Along with 'benching', 'ghosting' is another phenomenon said to be plaguing today's dating scene, which means communication is cut completely out of the blue, with the person seemingly disappearing.

A recent survey released by the dating site Plenty of Fish found that 78 per cent of single Millennials - or people between the age of 18 and 33 - have been 'ghosted' at least once.

Which means that over three quarters of men and women within that age bracket have had someone in their lives cease communication with them and leave altogether.

The term 'ghosting' first came to the fore in 2014, when the New York Times announced that the spooky term was a new entry into the dating lexicon.

While in 2014, a study by Elle US found that only 17 per cent of men and 24 per cent of women had been 'ghosted' at some point - right now that number has more than tripled.
