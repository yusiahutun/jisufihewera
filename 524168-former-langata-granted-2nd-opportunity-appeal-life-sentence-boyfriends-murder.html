Former Miss Lang'ata Granted 2nd Opportunity to Appeal Life Sentence in Boyfriend's Murder

  • Ruth Kamande was convicted of the gruesome murder of her boyfriend, Farid Mohamed Halim, in 2015
  • She was found guilty of stabbing him 25 times in their Buru Buru residence within Nairobi City county
  • In November 2020, Kamande’s previous appeal to overturn her conviction and sentence was unsuccessful

Nairobi - Former Miss Lang'ata Ruth Kamande, currently serving a death sentence in Lang’ata Women’s Prison, has been granted the opportunity to appeal her sentence at the Supreme Court.

This decision comes after the Court of Appeal rejected the beauty pageant winner's initial appeal.

Kamande was convicted of the gruesome murder of her boyfriend, Farid Mohamed Halim, in 2015.

She was found guilty of stabbing him 25 times in their Buru Buru residence, with witnesses reporting Halim’s cries for help before the police arrived, only to find him dead.

The post-mortem examination confirmed that he died from multiple injuries and blood loss from the penetrating force trauma inflicted upon him.

In November 2020, Kamande’s previous appeal to overturn her conviction and sentence was unsuccessful.

How Ruth Kamande's lawyer argued the case

Cape Media reported that her lawyer, Githu Muigai, argued that individuals who claim self-defence in cases of domestic violence should be considered for reduced charges.

While Muigai’s previous argument for a lesser charge of manslaughter based on self-defence given the nod was unsuccessful, he now asserts that this new appeal raises crucial issues related to battered women syndrome and the standards of proof for self-defence claims.

The submission also emphasises the importance of protecting and advancing the rights of domestic violence victims in line with the Protection Against Domestic Violence Act.

Kamande argues that the prosecution did not adequately challenge her claim during her trial, and the court did not adequately consider it.

Surprisingly, the prosecution did not oppose her application to appeal, believing it is in the public interest to examine the raised issues more extensively at the Supreme Court.

What is the concept of battered women's syndrome

The Court of Appeal agreed with this perspective, considering the grounds for appeal as potential novel issues of law deserving the Supreme Court’s attention.

The court acknowledged that the concept of battered women syndrome is relatively unexplored in the country, making it a matter of significant public interest.

Why Eric Omondi rescued convicted women

Earlier, TUKO.co.ke reported that petty offenders freed from Lang'ata Women's Prison narrated their tough experiences serving time incarcerated.

With tears freely rolling down their faces, the women stated that they did tough, menial jobs that led to their hands getting hurt.
