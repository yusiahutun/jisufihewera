NYC 'wolverine' Frank Cavalluzzi is convicted of attempted murder after driving into BLM protesters

Frank Cavalluzzi, who became known as the 'Queens Wolverine' has been convicted of attempted murder after he tried to plow his vehicle into a group of Black Lives Matter protestors and then threatened to kill them with a four blade knife-claw.

The unhinged Cavalluzzi, 57, was convicted on Friday of nine counts of attempted murder in the second degree and nine counts of attempted assault in the first degree in the heinous June 2, 2020 attack that took place in Whitestone.

Other charges include seven counts of menacing in the second degree, and criminal possession of a weapon in the fourth degree and reckless driving. 

He faces up to 25 years in prison. His sentencing date set forth by Queens Supreme Court Justice Michelle A. Johnson is October 13.

Queens District Attorney Melinda Katz, said 'a dangerous man is going to jail. It's a good day for New York and the First Amendment.'

Cavalluzzi became known as the 'Queens Wolverine' after brandishing a four blade knife-claw. He was convicted on Friday of nine counts of attempted murder in the second degree and nine counts of attempted assault in the first degree in the heinous June 2, 2020 attack that took place in Whitestone

On the day of the attack, June 2, 2020, Cavalluzzi came up to a group of BLM protestors at the intersection of the Cross Island Parkway service road and Clintonville Street in Whitestone.

The demonstrators were holding up signs during the peaceful demonstration.  

Cavalluzi abruptly stopped his vehicle and went on a tirade screaming profanities and racial slurs, including, 'You are in the wrong neighborhood.'

He then made a U-turn, exited his SUV and chased several of the demonstrators with a bladed leather glove that had been strapped to his right arm and had four serrated blades attached to them. 

When he re-entered his vehicle, he yelled out, 'I will kill you,' before driving onto the sidewalk where the protestors were standing, according to the charges and trial testimony from the Queens District Attorney's office.

Miraculously, no one was injured in the unprovoked attack.  

Detective Jason Hubbard of the New York City's Police Department 109th Pct. led the investigation.

In an exclusive video for DailyMail.com, our reporter asked Cavalluzzi, who was wearing a tie dye long sleeve shirt and clutching a Trader Joe's shopping bag, if he wanted to say anything in his own defense.

Cavalluzzi did not comment. 

As he leaves the court room, this time wearing a different t-shirt, a Jimi Hendrix tee, and his cane wielding lawyer, our reporter asked Cavalluzzi again if there's anything that he would like to say. He again ignores our reporter.

Cavalluzzi's lawyer then bizarrely asks our reporter, what photographers used to do with excess pictures they had after covering presidential election campaigns.

When the lawyer was asked, if he would like to make a comment on his client's case, he goes silent.

At that time, Cavalluzzi emerges from a group of armed US Marshals, wearing body armor, and begins to get threatening.

He tells our reporter: 'Come over, let's speak to me... stay the f**k away from me.'

Cavalluzzi, who lives in upstate New York, has business interests in Queens, and a long history of run-ins with the law. In this clip, he appeared irate and reportedly attacked a reporter

Cavalluzzi is seen pictured wearing a Jimi Hendrix black t-shirt in Queens Supreme Court on June 23, 2022,in what appears to be some type of smile underneath his mask, two years after the heinous attack was made on a group of peaceful Black Lives Matters protestors 

Cavalluzzi posted on Facebook that he had been robbed the day before his rampage

After the 2020 attack, Queens DA said:  'in a burst of anger and rage, this defendant allegedly sought to kill protesters who were peacefully assembled and exercising their right to free speech.'

'No one at any time should infringe upon another's Constitutionally-protected freedoms and doing so with the intent to injure and maim is criminal.'

Well-known to the police, and with a history of making threats on social media, Cavalluzzi's Facebook page shows his support for Donald Trump and a series of gun rights groups.

In the week prior to his attack he seemed to be growing increasingly furious as protests mounted, tweeting: 'I still have that bullet in the chamber, and several others for the rest of the scum trying to take down my constitution'.

The day before the rampage  Cavaluzzi posted on Facebook that his van was burglarized and $3,000 worth of power tools were stolen. He threatened 'to burn their house to the ground.'

Cavalluzzi was increasingly angered by the protests which had sprung up since George Floyd's killing in May 2020

Even before the killing of Floyd, Cavalluzzi was posting in fury against the government, railing against what he said was an attempt to take away Americans' guns

Cavalluzzi, who lives in upstate New York, has business interests in Queens, and a long history of run-ins with the law.

He has eight prior arrests dating back to 1988, the New York Post reported, and was most recently detained on January 22, 2016 for assaulting a police officer, resisting arrest and obstruction during a traffic stop on the Upper East Side.   

That incident bears strong parallels to Tuesday's attack: he is accused of exiting his vehicle, acting erratically and running and screaming towards the officer’s vehicle before attacking the patrol-person.

In April 2012 he was charged with menacing with a weapon in the second degree after he punched his female landlord, displayed a knife and again, threatened officers, police said.

In February 2004 he was arrested for intentionally breaking the rear window of a vehicle and resisting arrest during a road rage incident.

Back in September 1998, he was charged with harassment, assault causing injury with a weapon and for leaving the scene of an accident in another road rage dispute.

In April 2021, Cavalluzzi pleaded not guilty to the charges against him and was released on a $100,000 bail.
