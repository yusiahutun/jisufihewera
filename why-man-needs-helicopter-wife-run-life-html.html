Why every man needs a helicopter wife to run his life

By Rachel Halliwell for MailOnline
Updated: 05:28 EDT, 6 October 2011

103

View
comments

Last weekend was our wedding anniversary. When I awoke and my husband handed me a neatly wrapped package, I didn’t need to open it to know that what lay inside would be something I’d hankered after for ages.

It was a Kindle — the exact model I’ve been admiring. Am I blessed with a mind-reader as a husband? Well, no. Actually I had logged on to his Amazon account and ordered it, before handing it over to him to wrap on Friday evening.

‘It’s not very romantic,’ he moaned. ‘Why couldn’t you have trusted me to pick something?’

Piloting the marriage: Helicopter wives hover over their husbands and attempt to manipulate his every move (posed by model)

Poor thing. Little did he realise 19 years ago, as he watched me walk down the aisle towards him, that the smiling vision approaching was about to turn into a helicopter wife, hovering over him and attempting to manipulate his every move before the thank-you cards for our wedding gifts had even been posted.

That’s right — helicopter wife. We’re more used to hearing about helicopter mothers. But trust me, helicopter wives exist in equal abundance — heck, dear reader, you might even be one.

We’re the kind of women who keep our husbands’ diaries and train them to say ‘I’ or ‘we’ but think ‘she’ whenever there’s a decision to be made.

We think nothing of booking the family holiday without consulting anyone other than tripadvisor.com first. And we’re expert at manipulating our other half’s social life so he finds himself hanging out with the men who are married to our girlfriends rather than any mates of his own.

The helicopter wife means well, of course. She knows that were it not for her ‘help’ her man would still spend his free time sitting on the sofa playing Xbox, only buying new pants after the old ones finally disintegrated in the wash. 

Frankly, helicopter wife’s husband has relinquished far more control than he ever deserved to have in the first place.

Deep down he now knows as well as she does that were it not for her organising regular weekend lunches, he’d hardly ever socialise outside work.

Godchildren’s birthdays would come and go without a card arriving in the post. Mother’s Day would come as an annual heart-stopping surprise.

Of course, he’d like to pick out Christmas presents for the children himself, and be able to make executive decisions about how to spend his spare time. But he knows that if he makes a fuss when his missus has gone to so much trouble to organise things, he’ll be made to feel like the most ungrateful wretch walking the planet.

Meanwhile, helicopter wife sighs when her husband phones from work to ask if she’ll make him an appointment with the doctor. ‘Couldn’t you have booked it yourself, instead of phoning me?’

But such refrains are just an act, as I know that these favours add up, making it harder for him to fight back when I next slip into helicopter mode. 

The helicopter wife is expert at forging her husband’s signature on cards so that she can send gifts in his name, and knows all her husband’s passwords off by heart.

‘How come my work dinner is on the big calendar in the kitchen?’ my hubby asked the other day. ‘I didn’t put it there.’ I cheerfully admitted that I’d transferred it from his diary because I didn’t trust him to do it. 

I also open his post to make sure his credit card bills gets paid on time and his pension plan is performing OK.

In fact, there is only one area that my husband won’t allow me to control — his wardrobe. 

‘At least let me dress myself!’ he protests. ‘Darling, whatever do you mean?’ I ask, before quietly logging back on to his Amazon account and checking out what lovely present he’ll be buying me for my birthday, even if he doesn’t know it yet.
